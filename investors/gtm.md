# Orient Go-to-Market Strategy

## Investor Strategy Document

## 1. Executive summary

Orient is a shared meaning system for humans and machines.

It has two connected products:

1. **Orient for Humans**
   A thinking and communication space where teams capture knowledge, ask questions, resolve answers, create frames, share understanding, and build organisational memory.

2. **Orient for Machines**
   A machine-ready meaning layer that allows agents, assistants, workflows, copilots, and applications to consume the organisation’s resolved knowledge, context, frames, and memory.

The strategic insight is simple:

**Humans feed the graph. Machines act from it. Orient keeps both working from the same understanding.**

Orient enters the market at a moment when companies are deploying more AI tools, copilots, and agents, but those systems still lack the organisational meaning they need to act safely and usefully.

They can generate, search, summarise, and automate.

But they do not know:

* what the organisation currently believes;
* what evidence supports a claim;
* what is contested;
* what has already been decided;
* what is stale;
* what is still open;
* what should not be claimed;
* what people understand;
* when an agent should stop, ask, or escalate.

Orient solves this by turning human work into a living meaning graph: claims, evidence, contradictions, answers, open probes, frames, decisions, topics, people, comprehension, memory, and safe-to-act context.

The go-to-market strategy is to land first with high-value human use cases where knowledge is complex and decisions matter, then expand into machine access as the organisation begins to use agents and AI workflows.

The commercial model follows the product architecture:

**Orient for Humans** is sold by seat.
**Orient for Machines** is sold by dependency: platform fee, machine consumers, and meaning consumption.

This creates a strong expansion path:

1. Land in one strategic team.
2. Expand to multiple human teams.
3. Add machine access for agents and workflows.
4. Become the organisation’s shared meaning layer.

Orient should not be positioned as a normal collaboration tool, knowledge base, or RAG product.

It should be positioned as:

**the meaning layer between human knowledge and machine action.**

# 2. Market context

Organisations are entering a new phase of software.

The last generation of tools helped people store, search, organise, and produce information.

The next generation of systems will act on information.

Companies are already adopting:

* copilots;
* AI assistants;
* research agents;
* sales agents;
* support agents;
* workflow bots;
* coding agents;
* enterprise search agents;
* internal knowledge agents;
* automation systems.

But these systems face the same problem.

They can act before they understand.

Most AI systems rely on retrieval. They search files, pull chunks, and generate plausible answers. But organisations do not operate on chunks. They operate on meaning: what is true, what is supported, what is contested, what has been decided, what is safe to say, and what remains unresolved.

This creates a new market need:

**Companies need a shared meaning layer that both people and machines can use.**

Orient is built for that need.

# 18. Risks and mitigations

## Risk 1: Category is abstract

Mitigation:

Sell concrete use cases first: board prep, product launch, sales positioning, research-to-action, AI strategy.

## Risk 2: Product is too broad

Mitigation:

Lead with the core loop: Capture → Resolve → Frame → Measure → Remember.

Use a specific workspace as the entry point.

## Risk 3: Machine layer is ahead of customer readiness

Mitigation:

Sell Orient for Humans first, then introduce machine access where customers already have agents or clear agent plans.

## Risk 4: Customers compare Orient to knowledge bases or RAG

Mitigation:

Use the sharp contrast:

RAG gives chunks. Orient gives meaning.

Show current answers, evidence, contradictions, open probes, and safe-to-act signals.

## Risk 5: Enterprise sales cycles are long

Mitigation:

Start with high-value founder-led design partners and team-level deployments.

Land with a strategic project rather than an enterprise-wide rollout.

## Risk 6: Meaning quality must be high

Mitigation:

Make human review central. Machines may propose meaning; humans confirm meaning.

## Risk 7: Machine writeback could poison the graph

Mitigation:

Start read-only. Add guarded writeback later with review, provenance, confidence discounting, and audit.
